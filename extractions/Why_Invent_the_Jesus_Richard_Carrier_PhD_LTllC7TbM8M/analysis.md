# Content Analysis: Why Invent the Jesus? (Richard Carrier, PhD)

## Overview
- Carrier argues that earliest Christian sources describe a celestial Christ revealed through visions, while biographical gospels arose later as allegorical literature.
- He proposes that Jesus was invented by adapting Jewish angelology and Greco-Roman savior cult motifs to replace the Jerusalem temple’s atonement system.
- The talk surveys how myth-making, forgery, and political consolidation “historicized” Jesus for mass audiences.

## Key Findings

### Sparse early evidence points to a visionary cult
- The authentic Pauline letters (our first Christian texts) depict revelations and lack any ministry narrative; later gospels appear a lifetime afterward and are already mythic, with external attestations derivative of them (`extractions/Why_Invent_the_Jesus_Richard_Carrier_PhD_LTllC7TbM8M/Why_Invent_the_Jesus_Richard_Carrier_PhD_LTllC7TbM8M.en.txt:60`).
- Carrier stresses the absence of contemporary corroboration and the prevalence of forgery in early Christian literature, indicating a heavily curated archive (`...en.txt:129`).

### Pre-existing archangel provides the template
- Some Jews had already identified a heavenly Jesus as God’s firstborn archangel; Christians taught that this angel descended, was killed, and resurrected—mirroring and reversing Satan’s myth (`...en.txt:189`).
- Texts like the *Ascension of Isaiah* show strands where Jesus is slain by demons “above the clouds,” supporting a celestial crucifixion motif later overwritten by earthly biography (`...en.txt:245`).

### Pauline “brothers” and birth language are ambiguous
- References to “brothers of the Lord” can denote cultic kinship rather than biological family; Paul never clarifies otherwise (`...en.txt:383`).
- Greek terms translated as “born” or “descended” signify divine manufacture (used for Adam and resurrection bodies), suggesting theological production rather than literal birth (`...en.txt:427`).

### Sectarian battles drove historicization
- Forged letters like 2 Peter explicitly counter Christians who called Jesus “a cleverly devised myth,” implying intra-Christian disputes over historicity (`...en.txt:645`).
- Church leaders such as Origen justified feeding the masses literal tales while keeping allegorical interpretations for insiders, showing deliberate popularization of a historicized narrative (`...en.txt:680`).
- With Christians few in number (Pliny’s testimony) and documents under later imperial control, a literalist faction could overwrite rival traditions (`...en.txt:728`).

### Savior-god pattern and Jewish adaptation
- Carrier situates Jesus among numerous dying-and-rising savior gods (Osiris, Adonis, Inanna, etc.) who granted immortality via ritual communion, arguing Jesus would be the anomaly if historical (`...en.txt:848`).
- The gospels recast Yom Kippur and Passover sacrifices in allegorical stories (e.g., Barabbas and Jesus as the two goats), portraying Jesus as the temple-replacing atonement (`...en.txt:1041`, `...en.txt:1320`).

### Gospels as allegorical scripture
- Mark flags his narrative as parabolic; episodes like the fig tree curse allegorize God’s rejection of the Jerusalem temple rather than report literal events (`...en.txt:1144`).
- Gospel pericopes provide missionary script, ritual explanations, and apostolic pedigrees to curb rival visionaries, framing biography as institutional propaganda rather than memory (`...en.txt:1350`, `...en.txt:1381`).

## Conclusions
- Carrier’s thesis presents Jesus as a synthetic savior figure crafted from Jewish angelology, temple critique, and broader ancient salvation cult motifs, later literalized to bolster institutional authority.
- The earliest extant evidence is consistent with a visionary mystery religion; historicizing biographies emerge amid theological competition and deliberate narrative control.
- Evaluating Christian origins thus requires parsing allegorical gospels and forged texts within a landscape of myth-making common to the era.
